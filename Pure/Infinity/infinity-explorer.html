The Endless Number Line

Core idea: Integers never end. No matter how large a number you name, you can always add one more. Infinity is not a number — it describes an unending process.

Think about it

What is the largest integer?
There is no largest integer. For any integer \(N\), the integer \(N+1\) is larger. The process of adding 1 never terminates — this is what mathematicians mean when they say the integers are infinite.
Is infinity a number?
No. \(\infty\) is not a real number — it is a concept describing an unending process or an unbounded quantity. You cannot substitute \(\infty\) into arithmetic operations the way you can substitute a real number. Expressions like \(\infty - \infty\) or \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\) are indeterminate and have no single value.
Are there different "sizes" of infinity?
Yes — Georg Cantor showed this in the 19th century. The integers and the rational numbers are both "countably infinite" (the same size), but the real numbers form a strictly larger "uncountably infinite" set. There are infinitely many different sizes of infinity!

Think about it

Does 1/n ever equal zero?
No. For any finite positive integer \(n\), \(\frac{1}{n} > 0\). The sequence approaches zero as \(n \to \infty\), which we write \(\frac{1}{n} \to 0\), but no term in the sequence actually equals zero.
Which shrinks faster: 1/n or 1/n²?
\(\frac{1}{n^2}\) shrinks much faster. For example, at \(n=100\): \(\frac{1}{100} = 0.01\) but \(\frac{1}{100^2} = 0.0001\). Formally, \(\frac{1/n^2}{1/n} = \frac{1}{n} \to 0\), so \(1/n^2\) is said to be of smaller order than \(1/n\).

Think about it

Why is 0/0 called "indeterminate"?
Because different limits of the form \(\frac{f(x)}{g(x)}\) where \(f,g\to 0\) can give any value — zero, a finite non-zero number, or infinity. The symbol \(\frac{0}{0}\) by itself carries no information about what the limit is; you must examine the specific functions involved.
What other indeterminate forms exist?
The classic indeterminate forms are: \(\frac{0}{0}\), \(\frac{\infty}{\infty}\), \(0 \cdot \infty\), \(\infty - \infty\), \(0^0\), \(1^\infty\), and \(\infty^0\). Each requires careful analysis — usually L'Hôpital's rule or algebraic manipulation — to find the limit.
Module 4

What is a Limit?

Core idea: We write \(\lim_{n \to \infty} a_n = L\) and say the limit of \(a_n\) is \(L\) if we can make \(a_n\) as close to \(L\) as we wish by taking \(n\) sufficiently large. This is made precise by the \(\varepsilon\text{-}N\) definition.
\(\displaystyle \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n = L \iff \forall\,\varepsilon > 0,\ \exists\, N \in \mathbb{N} :\ n > N \Rightarrow |a_n - L| < \varepsilon\)
In plain English: No matter how small a "tolerance" \(\varepsilon\) you specify around \(L\), all terms of the sequence after some index \(N\) must lie within that tolerance.
0.30
All terms inside the ε-band after N.

Think about it

Does the limit have to be reached?
No. The limit \(L\) is the value the sequence approaches, not necessarily a value the sequence attains. For example, \(a_n = \frac{1}{n}\) has limit 0 but never equals 0.
Can a sequence fail to have a limit?
Yes. A sequence diverges if it grows without bound (e.g. \(a_n = n\)), or if it oscillates without settling (e.g. \(a_n = (-1)^n\)). Only sequences that eventually "settle down" near a single value possess a limit.

Think about it

Does a bounded sequence always converge?
No. The sequence \(a_n = (-1)^n\) is bounded (\(|a_n| = 1\) for all \(n\)) but does not converge because it oscillates between \(+1\) and \(-1\). However, a bounded monotone sequence always converges — this is the Monotone Convergence Theorem.
When does a geometric sequence converge?
A geometric sequence \(a_n = a \cdot r^{n-1}\) converges if and only if \(|r| < 1\) (in which case \(a_n \to 0\)) or \(a = 0\). If \(|r| \geq 1\) and \(a \neq 0\), the sequence diverges.

Think about it

If the terms → 0, does the series converge?
Not necessarily! The harmonic series \(\sum \frac{1}{n}\) is the classic counterexample: its terms \(\frac{1}{n} \to 0\) but the series still diverges. Terms approaching zero is a necessary condition for convergence, but not sufficient.
What is the sum of the geometric series \(\sum_{n=0}^\infty \left(\frac{1}{2}\right)^n\)?
Using the formula \(\frac{a}{1-r}\) with \(a=1, r=\frac{1}{2}\): \(\frac{1}{1-\frac{1}{2}} = 2\). This is Zeno's paradox in a formula — infinitely many positive terms sum to a finite value!

Why Convergence Matters

Core idea: Convergent series let us compute exact values — like \(\pi\), \(e\), and \(\ln 2\) — as infinite sums. They underpin calculus, Fourier analysis, and much of modern mathematics and physics.

Think about it

What is the Basel problem?
The Basel problem asks for the exact value of \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{n^2} = 1 + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{9} + \cdots\). Euler solved it in 1734, showing the answer is \(\frac{\pi^2}{6} \approx 1.6449\). The appearance of \(\pi\) in a sum involving only integers was astounding.
Why does e appear everywhere?
Euler's number \(e = \sum_{n=0}^\infty \frac{1}{n!} = 1 + 1 + \frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{6} + \cdots \approx 2.718\). It arises naturally as the base of natural logarithms, in compound interest, in differential equations, and throughout probability theory — because the exponential function \(e^x\) is its own derivative.

Convergence Tests

Core idea: Given a series \(\sum a_n\), how do we tell if it converges? A toolkit of tests covers most cases you'll meet at A-level and beyond.

Divergence Test (nth-term test)

\(\text{If } \lim_{n\to\infty} a_n \neq 0, \text{ then } \sum a_n \text{ diverges.}\)
Warning: If \(a_n \to 0\), the test is inconclusive — the series might converge (geometric) or diverge (harmonic). The test only proves divergence.

• If \(\sum b_n\) converges → \(\sum a_n\) converges
• If \(\sum a_n\) diverges → \(\sum b_n\) diverges

Module 9

Infinity Paradoxes

Core idea: Infinity leads to results that feel paradoxical but are mathematically precise. Understanding these paradoxes deepens intuition for limits, series, and the foundations of analysis.

Zeno's Dichotomy Paradox

To cross a room you must first cross half of it, then half of what remains, then half again… There are infinitely many steps. How can you ever arrive?
\(\frac{1}{2} + \frac{1}{4} + \frac{1}{8} + \cdots = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n} = 1\)
Resolution: An infinite number of steps need not take infinite time if each step takes proportionally less time. The geometric series \(\sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{1}{2^n} = 1\) converges — infinitely many terms sum to a finite value. Zeno's error was assuming infinitely many steps must take infinite time.

Hilbert's Infinite Hotel

Imagine a hotel with infinitely many rooms, all occupied. Can it accommodate a new guest?
Yes! Move every guest from room \(n\) to room \(n+1\). Room 1 is now free. In fact, the hotel can accommodate infinitely many new guests: move guest in room \(n\) to room \(2n\), freeing all odd-numbered rooms for the new arrivals. This illustrates that \(\infty + 1 = \infty\) and \(\infty + \infty = \infty\) for countably infinite sets.
\(|\mathbb{N}| = |\mathbb{N} \cup \{0\}| = |\mathbb{Z}| = |\mathbb{Q}| = \aleph_0\)
Cantor's insight: Two sets have the same "size" (cardinality) if their elements can be paired one-to-one. The integers, even numbers, and rationals all pair perfectly with the natural numbers — they're all the same infinity \(\aleph_0\). But the real numbers cannot be paired with \(\mathbb{N}\) — they form a strictly larger infinity \(\aleph_1 = 2^{\aleph_0}\).

Grandi's Series

\(S = 1 - 1 + 1 - 1 + 1 - \cdots\)   What is the sum?
\(S_N = \begin{cases} 1 & N \text{ odd} \\ 0 & N \text{ even}\end{cases}\)
The partial sums oscillate between 0 and 1 and never converge — so by the standard definition, this series diverges. However, some summation methods (Cesàro, Abel) assign it the value \(\frac{1}{2}\). This illustrates that the "sum" of a series depends critically on the definition of summation used, and why rigorous definitions matter in mathematics.

Think about it

Is 0.999… = 1?
Yes, exactly. \(0.\overline{9} = \sum_{n=1}^\infty \frac{9}{10^n} = \frac{9/10}{1-1/10} = 1\). They are different decimal representations of the same real number, just as \(\frac{1}{2} = \frac{2}{4}\). The apparent "paradox" arises from confusing the decimal representation with the number itself.
